Cramer's Rule

Course Objective

To help learners understand and apply Cramer's Rule to solve systems of linear equations using determinants.

Cramer's Rule is a method for solving systems of linear equations using matrices and determinants. It applies only when:

  • The system has the same number of equations and unknowns (a square system).
  • The determinant of the coefficient matrix is not zero.

üí° If the determinant is 0, Cramer's Rule cannot be used.


üìç Lesson 1: Review of Determinants (2√ó2 and 3√ó3)

‚úÖ 2√ó2 Matrix

For matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \]

The determinant is:

\[ \text{det}(A) = ad - bc \]

‚úÖ 3√ó3 Matrix

For matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{bmatrix} \]

The determinant is:

\[ \text{det}(A) = a(ei - fh) - b(di - fg) + c(dh - eg) \]

üìç Lesson 2: Applying Cramer's Rule (2√ó2 system)

‚úèÔ∏è Example:

Solve this system:

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x + 3y &= 5 \\ 4x - y &= 11 \end{aligned} \]

Step 1: Coefficient Matrix

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{det}(A) = (2)(-1) - (4)(3) = -2 - 12 = -14 \]

Step 2: Replace columns

For x: Replace the first column with constants

\[ A_x = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 \\ 11 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{det}(A_x) = (5)(-1) - (11)(3) = -5 - 33 = -38 \]

For y: Replace the second column

\[ A_y = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 \\ 4 & 11 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{det}(A_y) = (2)(11) - (4)(5) = 22 - 20 = 2 \]

Step 3: Divide

\[ x = \frac{\text{det}(A_x)}{\text{det}(A)} = \frac{-38}{-14} = \frac{19}{7} \quad ; \quad y = \frac{2}{-14} = -\frac{1}{7} \]

‚úÖ Final Answer:

\[ x = \frac{19}{7}, \quad y = -\frac{1}{7} \]

üìç Lesson 3: Cramer's Rule for 3√ó3 Systems

The steps are the same:

  1. Compute determinant of coefficient matrix (D)
  2. Replace each column with constants to get D‚Çì, D·µß, Dùìè
  3. Solve:
\[ x = \frac{D_x}{D}, \\quad y = \frac{D_y}{D}, \\quad z = \frac{D_z}{D} \]

Summary Cheatsheet

  • Cramer's Rule = Determinant-based method
  • Only works for square systems with non-zero determinant
  • Replace columns with constant matrix to solve for each variable
  • Great for 2√ó2 or 3√ó3 systems

Practice Problems

  1. Solve: \[ \begin{aligned} 3x - y &= 7 \\ 2x + 4y &= 10 \end{aligned} \]
  2. Solve: \[ \begin{aligned} x + y + z &= 6 \\ 2x - y + 3z &= 14 \\ -x + 2y - z &= -2 \end{aligned} \]

Gauss Elimination

Course Objective

To teach learners how to solve systems of linear equations by transforming them into row echelon form (REF) using Gauss Elimination, incorporating a self-made formula to better understand.

Formula:

\[ \left( \frac{R_n}{R_m} \right)(\text{Eq}_m) - \text{Eq}_n \]

Where:

  • R‚Çô is the pivot element in the target row Eq‚Çô
  • R‚Çò is the pivot element in the reference row Eq‚Çò
  • This gives a scalar multiple of Eq‚Çò to eliminate a variable in Eq‚Çô

This is essentially a structured way of saying: Make the leading entry (pivot) 1, and eliminate the entries below it.


üìç Lesson 1: Steps & Formula in Action

‚úèÔ∏è Example System:

\[ \begin{aligned} 2x - y + 6z &= 10 \\ -3x + 4y - 5z &= 11 \\ 8x - 7y - 9z &= 12 \end{aligned} \]

Step 1: Augmented Matrix

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 6 & | & 10 \\ -3 & 4 & -5 & | & 11 \\ 8 & -7 & -9 & | & 12 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Make the pivot in Row 1 a 1

Divide Row 1 by 2:

\[ R_1 \rightarrow \frac{1}{2} R_1 = [1, -0.5, 3, | 5] \]

Step 3: Eliminate entries below the pivot (1st column)

To eliminate a‚ÇÇ‚ÇÅ (in Row 2):

\[ \left( \frac{-3}{1} \right) R_1 - R_2 = (-3)[1, -0.5, 3, 5] - [-3, 4, -5, 11] \]

Compute:

\[ [-3, 1.5, -9, -15] - [-3, 4, -5, 11] = [0, -2.5, -4, -26] \]

‚úÖ New Row 2: [0, -2.5, -4, -26]

To eliminate a‚ÇÉ‚ÇÅ (in Row 3):

\[ \left( \frac{8}{1} \right) R_1 - R_3 = (8)[1, -0.5, 3, 5] - [8, -7, -9, 12] \]

Compute:

\[ [8, -4, 24, 40] - [8, -7, -9, 12] = [0, 3, 33, 28] \]

‚úÖ New Row 3: [0, 3, 33, 28]

Updated Matrix:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 & 3 & | & 5 \\ 0 & -2.5 & -4 & | & -26 \\ 0 & 3 & 33 & | & 28 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 4: Make pivot in Row 2 a 1

\[ R_2 \rightarrow \frac{1}{-2.5} R_2 = [0, 1, 1.6, | 10.4] \]

Step 5: Eliminate below pivot (Row 3, Column 2)

\[ \left( \frac{3}{1} \right) R_2 - R_3 = 3[0, 1, 1.6, 10.4] - [0, 3, 33, 28] \]

Compute:

\[ [0, 3, 4.8, 31.2] - [0, 3, 33, 28] = [0, 0, -28.2, 3.2] \]

‚úÖ New Row 3: [0, 0, -28.2, 3.2]

Step 6: Back-substitution

Final Row Echelon Form:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 & 3 & | & 5 \\ 0 & 1 & 1.6 & | & 10.4 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & | & -\frac{3.2}{28.2} \end{bmatrix} \]

You can then work backward to find z, y, and x.

Summary Cheatsheet

  • Normalize the pivot row (make leading 1)
  • Eliminate below using: \(\left( \frac{R_n}{R_m} \right)(\text{Eq}_m) - \text{Eq}_n\)
  • Continue down the matrix
  • Use back-substitution to solve

Gauss-Jordan Elimination

Course Objective

To teach learners how to solve a system of linear equations by transforming it into Reduced Row Echelon Form (RREF) using Gauss-Jordan Elimination, integrating a self-made formula:

\[ \left( \frac{R_n}{R_m} \right)(\text{Eq}_m) - \text{Eq}_n \]

üß† What's the Difference from Gauss Elimination?

  • Gauss Elimination gives you row echelon form (triangular), and you solve by back-substitution.
  • Gauss-Jordan Elimination continues until you get reduced row echelon form (like an identity matrix), solving the system directly without substitution.

üîç Steps of Gauss-Jordan Elimination:

  1. Make the pivot element = 1
  2. Use your formula to eliminate both below and above the pivot
  3. Repeat for the next pivot to eventually get identity matrix on the left

üìç Lesson 1: Step-by-Step Solving with Gauss-Jordan

Write the Augmented Matrix

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 6 & | & 10 \\ -3 & 4 & -5 & | & 11 \\ 8 & -7 & -9 & | & 12 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Make pivot at Row 1, Col 1 a 1

\[ R_1 \rightarrow \frac{1}{2} R_1 = [1, -0.5, 3, | 5] \]

Step 3: Eliminate entries above and below the pivot using:

\[ \left( \frac{R_n}{R_m} \right)(\text{Eq}_m) - \text{Eq}_n \]

Eliminate Row 2 (entry = -3):

\[ \left( \frac{-3}{1} \right) R_1 - R_2 = -3 R_1 - R_2\\ [-3, 1.5, -9, -15] - [-3, 4, -5, 11] = [0, -2.5, -4, -26] \]

Eliminate Row 3 (entry = 8):

\[ \left( \frac{8}{1} \right) R_1 - R_3 = 8 R_1 - R_3\\ [8, -4, 24, 40] - [8, -7, -9, 12] = [0, 3, 33, 28] \]

Matrix After 1st Pivot Operation:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 & 3 & | & 5 \\ 0 & -2.5 & -4 & | & -26 \\ 0 & 3 & 33 & | & 28 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 4: Make pivot in Row 2, Col 2 a 1

\[ R_2 \rightarrow \frac{1}{-2.5} R_2 = [0, 1, 1.6, | 10.4] \]

Step 5: Eliminate above and below this pivot (column 2)

Eliminate Row 1 (entry = -0.5):

\[ \left( \frac{-0.5}{1} \right) R_2 - R_1 = -0.5 R_2 - R_1\\ [0, -0.5, -0.8, -5.2] - [1, -0.5, 3, 5] = [1, 0, 3.8, 10.2] \]

Eliminate Row 3 (entry = 3):

\[ \left( \frac{3}{1} \right) R_2 - R_3 = 3 R_2 - R_3\\ [0, 3, 4.8, 31.2] - [0, 3, 33, 28] = [0, 0, -28.2, 3.2] \]

Updated Matrix:

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 3.8 & | & 10.2 \\ 0 & 1 & 1.6 & | & 10.4 \\ 0 & 0 & -28.2 & | & 3.2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 6: Make pivot in Row 3, Col 3 a 1

\[ R_3 \rightarrow \frac{1}{-28.2} R_3 = [0, 0, 1, | -\frac{3.2}{28.2}] \]

Step 7: Eliminate above (Rows 1 and 2)

Eliminate Row 1 (entry = 3.8):

\[ \left( \frac{3.8}{1} \right) R_3 - R_1 = 3.8 R_3 - R_1\\ [0, 0, 3.8, -0.431] - [1, 0, 3.8, 10.2] = [1, 0, 0, 9.769] \]

Eliminate Row 2 (entry = 1.6):

\[ 1.6 R_3 - R_2 = [0, 0, 1.6, -0.181] - [0, 1, 1.6, 10.4] = [0, 1, 0, 10.219] \]

Final Matrix (Reduced Row Echelon Form):

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & | & \approx 9.77 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & | & \approx 10.22 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & | & \approx -0.11 \end{bmatrix} \]

‚úÖ Final Answer:

\[ x \approx 9.77, \quad y \approx 10.22, \quad z \approx -0.11 \]

üßæ Summary of Steps

  1. Make pivot = 1
  2. Eliminate all other entries in that column (above and below) using: \(\left( \frac{R_n}{R_m} \right)(\text{Eq}_m) - \text{Eq}_n\)
  3. Repeat for each pivot column
  4. The matrix should now look like an identity matrix on the left

LU Decomposition

Course Objective

To teach you how to solve a system of linear equations by factoring the coefficient matrix A into the product of a Lower triangular matrix L and an Upper triangular matrix U such that:

\[ A = LU \]

Then solve:

  1. LUx = b
  2. Let Ux = y, then solve:
    • Ly = b (Forward Substitution)
    • Ux = y (Backward Substitution)

üîç When to Use LU Decomposition?

  • For solving multiple systems with the same coefficient matrix A but different right-hand sides b
  • It reduces computation time since you only decompose A once

üìç Lesson 1: What Is LU Decomposition?

Given:

\[ A \cdot x = b \]

You decompose:

\[ A = LU \\ \Rightarrow LUx = b \]

Let Ux = y, then:

  • Step 1: Solve Ly = b (forward)
  • Step 2: Solve Ux = y (backward)

üìç Lesson 2: LU Decomposition Process

We want:

\[ A = LU \\ \text{Where:} \\ \cdot L \text{ is lower triangular (with 1s on the diagonal)}\\ \cdot U \text{ is upper triangular (result of Gaussian Elimination)} \]

Step 1: Start with U = A, and L = I (identity matrix)

We'll perform Gaussian elimination on A, and track the multipliers used to eliminate elements ‚Äî they become the entries of L.

Step 2: Eliminate entries below the pivot (Gaussian-style)

Pivot at Row 1: Eliminate a‚ÇÇ‚ÇÅ and a‚ÇÉ‚ÇÅ

  • Multiplier for Row 2: \[ m_{21} = \frac{-3}{2} = -1.5 \\ \Rightarrow R_2 \leftarrow R_2 + 1.5 \cdot R_1 \]

    Update U:

    \[ R_2 = [-3, 4, -5] + 1.5 \cdot [2, -1, 6] = [0, 2.5, 4] \]

    Store multiplier in L: L‚ÇÇ‚ÇÅ = -1.5

  • Multiplier for Row 3: \[ m_{31} = \frac{8}{2} = 4 \\ \Rightarrow R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4 \cdot R_1 \]

    Update U:

    \[ R_3 = [8, -7, -9] - 4 \cdot [2, -1, 6] = [0, -3, -33] \]

    Store multiplier in L: L‚ÇÉ‚ÇÅ = 4

Step 3: Eliminate below Row 2 pivot (Column 2)

Pivot at U‚ÇÇ‚ÇÇ = 2.5

  • Multiplier for Row 3: \[ m_{32} = \frac{-3}{2.5} = -1.2 \\ \Rightarrow R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + 1.2 \cdot R_2 \]

    Update U:

    \[ R_3 = [0, -3, -33] + 1.2 \cdot [0, 2.5, 4] = [0, 0, -28.2] \]

    Store in L: L‚ÇÉ‚ÇÇ = -1.2

Final Matrices:

‚úÖ Upper Matrix U:

\[ U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 6 \\ 0 & 2.5 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & -28.2 \end{bmatrix} \]

‚úÖ Lower Matrix L:

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1.5 & 1 & 0 \\ 4 & -1.2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

üìç Lesson 3: Solve the System Using LUx = b

Step 1: Solve Ly = b (forward substitution)

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1.5 & 1 & 0 \\ 4 & -1.2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 11 \\ 12 \end{bmatrix} \]
  • y‚ÇÅ = 10
  • -1.5y‚ÇÅ + y‚ÇÇ = 11 \( \Rightarrow y_2 = 11 + 1.5(10) = 26 \)
  • 4y‚ÇÅ - 1.2y‚ÇÇ + y‚ÇÉ = 12 \( \Rightarrow y_3 = 12 - (4)(10) + (1.2)(26) = 12 - 40 + 31.2 = 3.2 \)

‚úÖ y = [10, 26, 3.2]

Step 2: Solve Ux = y (backward substitution)

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 6 \\ 0 & 2.5 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & -28.2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \\ 26 \\ 3.2 \end{bmatrix} \]
  • -28.2z = 3.2 \( \Rightarrow z = -\frac{3.2}{28.2} \approx -0.1135 \)
  • 2.5y + 4z = 26 \( \Rightarrow y = \frac{26 - 4(-0.1135)}{2.5} \approx 10.22 \)
  • 2x - y + 6z = 10 \( \Rightarrow x = \frac{10 + y - 6z}{2} \approx 9.77 \)

‚úÖ Final Answer:

\[ x \approx 9.77, \quad y \approx 10.22, \quad z \approx -0.11 \]

üìç Summary of LU Decomposition

Here's a quick recap of the LU Decomposition process:

Phase 1: Decompose

  • Apply Gaussian Elimination to form U
  • Track multipliers used to form L

Phase 2: Solve

  1. Ly = b (Forward Substitution)
  2. Ux = y (Backward Substitution)
